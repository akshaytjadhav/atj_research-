Chapter 1

What is a Research Paper?

Understanding research from scratch — as simple as it gets.

Think of it like this

Analogy
Imagine you notice that plants grow faster near a music speaker. You experiment, measure, document your process, find results, and share your discovery with the world. That document — with your method, findings, and conclusion — is a research paper.

A research paper is a formal written document where a researcher presents:

What they studied

The problem or question they investigated

How they studied it

The method or experiment they used

What they found

The results and data they collected

What it means

Their interpretation and conclusion

Why Do People Publish Papers?

1
Share knowledge
If you solve a problem, others shouldn't have to solve it again from scratch.
2
Career advancement
In academia and research, publications are your "marks." More papers = better job, promotion, funding.
3
Prove credibility
A published paper says: "experts checked my work and it passed." It is peer-validated knowledge.
4
Get academic degree credit
Many universities require papers for PhD, MTech, or even BTech programs.
5
Attract funding
Grants, scholarships, and industry collaborations often require a publication record.
Common Myth
"Only PhD students or professors can publish research." — False. Undergraduate and postgraduate students regularly publish papers. As a BTech/BE or MTech student, you can absolutely publish your first paper.

The Publication Ecosystem — A Simple Map

Think of research like news. A journalist writes a story → an editor checks it → it gets published in a newspaper → readers read it → other journalists cite it. Research works the same way:

Researcher — does experiment, collects results
↓
Writes a paper — formats it per journal guidelines
↓
Submits to a journal or conference
↓
Peer review — experts in the field check the work
↓
Accepted & Published — world can read and cite it

Realistic Timeline

A typical research paper takes 3 to 18 months from writing to publication. Conferences are faster (2–4 months). Top journals can take 6–18 months. Do not be discouraged — this is completely normal.

Average: ~6 months for most indexed journals

Chapter 2

Types of Publications

Journals, conferences, patents, book chapters — what's the difference?

At a Glance

TypeWhat it isBest forPrestige
Research ArticleOriginal study with new experiments/findingsNew discoveriesHigh
Review PaperSummary and analysis of 50–200 existing papers on a topicUnderstanding a fieldHigh
Conference PaperShort paper presented at an eventBeginners, quick sharingMedium
Book ChapterOne chapter in an edited bookDetailed topic coverageMedium
PatentLegal protection for an inventionProduct/technology innovationDifferent scale

Research Article

The gold standard of research. You conduct original experiments, collect data, analyze it, and report findings that did not exist before. Example: "A new algorithm for detecting cracks in concrete bridges using image processing."

Typical length: 4,000–8,000 words. Contains: Abstract, Introduction, Literature Review, Methodology, Results, Discussion, Conclusion, References.

Review Paper

You read 50–200 papers on a topic, compare them, identify patterns, gaps, and summarize the current state of knowledge. You don't do new experiments — you synthesize existing ones.

Analogy
A review paper is like a movie critic who watches all films in a genre and writes a comprehensive report — "here's what worked, what didn't, what's missing." You become the expert who saves others from reading 100 papers.

Conference Paper

A shorter paper (4–8 pages typically) submitted to an event (conference). If accepted, you present it there — either in person or virtually. Great for beginners because the review process is faster and the bar is somewhat lower than top journals.

Think of a conference like a science fair for professionals. Everyone displays their work and discusses it together.

Book Chapter

An editor compiles a book on a topic (e.g., "Advances in Renewable Energy"). They invite researchers to contribute one chapter each. It's collaborative and usually targets a specific audience. Less rigorously peer-reviewed than journal articles.

Patent

A patent is not a research paper — it is a legal document filed with the government that gives you exclusive rights to use or sell your invention for 20 years. Requires: novelty, non-obviousness, industrial applicability.

Beginner Tip
In India, you can file a patent through the Indian Patent Office (IPO). Universities often help students file at reduced cost. A published patent is counted separately from journal publications.

Beginner Strategy
Start with a national or international conference paper. Once published, you can extend the work with more experiments and submit it as a journal article. This is a very common and accepted practice.
Chapter 3

Databases, Metrics & Indexing

Scopus, Web of Science, Impact Factor, H-index, Quartiles — decoded simply.

What is Indexing?

Analogy
Imagine thousands of books in a library with no catalog. Finding anything is impossible. A library catalog indexes the books — every book gets an entry with title, author, subject, location. Now you can find any book in seconds. Research databases do exactly this for papers: they catalog, organize, and make papers discoverable worldwide.

An indexed journal means a reputed database has accepted that journal into its system — verifying it meets quality standards.

The Major Databases

Scopus

Run by Elsevier. The world's largest abstract and citation database. Contains ~90 million records from ~26,000 peer-reviewed journals. If a journal is "Scopus indexed," it passed Elsevier's quality check. Very important in India for PhD criteria and university requirements.

Check at: scopus.com/sources

Web of Science (WoS)

Run by Clarivate Analytics. Older and considered slightly more prestigious than Scopus. Has three tiers:

SCI
Science Citation Index — the oldest, most prestigious list. Top journals only.
SCIE
SCI Expanded — broader, includes more journals. Good quality standard.
ESCI
Emerging Sources CI — newer journals being evaluated. Less prestigious but recognized.

UGC CARE

University Grants Commission — Consortium for Academic and Research Ethics. India-specific approved journal list. Important for Indian university PhD requirements and faculty promotions. Has two groups: Group I (whitelist of approved journals) and Group II (Scopus/WoS indexed journals automatically qualify).

Google Scholar

Free search engine for academic papers. Not a quality indexing system — it crawls and indexes everything including predatory journals, theses, preprints. Very useful for searching papers and tracking citations, but being on Google Scholar does NOT mean a journal is good quality.

Important Distinction
"My paper is on Google Scholar" ≠ "My paper is indexed in Scopus." These are very different things. Always verify Scopus/WoS indexing separately.

DOI — Digital Object Identifier

Analogy
Think of DOI like an Aadhar card number for a research paper. Every published paper gets a unique permanent ID like 10.1016/j.energy.2023.127895. No matter where the internet moves or URLs change, this DOI always takes you to that paper.

Format: always starts with 10. followed by numbers and slashes. You can look up any DOI at doi.org

Key Quality Metrics — Simply Explained

Impact Factor (IF)

Analogy
If 100 researchers cite papers from Nature magazine this year, and Nature published 50 papers last year, the Impact Factor is 100/50 = 2. A higher IF means more researchers found the journal's papers useful enough to reference. A journal with IF > 5 is considered quite good. IF > 10 is excellent.

Published by Clarivate annually in the "Journal Citation Reports." Only WoS-indexed journals get an official IF.

CiteScore

Scopus's version of Impact Factor. Calculated over 4 years instead of 2. Generally considered to give a more stable and fair measure. A CiteScore of 3.0 is decent; 5+ is strong; 10+ is excellent.

H-index (of an Author)

Analogy
If a researcher has an H-index of 15, it means they have 15 papers that have each been cited at least 15 times. An H-index of 20 as a professor is quite good. Nobel laureates often have H-index 50+. For a student, H-index 2–5 is a solid start.

This metric measures an individual researcher's productivity and impact together. A single highly-cited paper doesn't give a very high H-index.

Quartiles: Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4

Journals in Scopus are ranked by CiteScore within their subject category. They are then divided into 4 equal groups:

Q1
Top 25%
Q2
25–50%
Q3
50–75%
Q4
Bottom 25%
Analogy
Think of it like college rankings. If there are 100 engineering colleges in a state, Q1 journals are like the top 25 colleges — the most prestigious. Q4 journals are like the bottom 25. For career impact, aim for Q1 or Q2 journals in your field.

You can check quartile rankings at scimagojr.com (for Scopus) — it's free.

Chapter 4

Journals, Fees & Predatory Publishers

Free vs paid, open access, and how to spot fake journals.

Types of Journals by Cost

Subscription Journals
Readers pay to access papers (via library subscription). Author publishes for free or very low cost. Examples: IEEE journals, Elsevier journals with no APC.
Open Access (OA) Journals
Anyone can read papers for free online. Author (or institution) pays an "Article Processing Charge" (APC). Example: MDPI journals, PLOS ONE.
Hybrid Journals
Normally subscription-based, but authors can pay extra to make their specific article open access. Most Springer and Elsevier journals work this way.
Diamond/Platinum OA
Completely free for both readers AND authors. Often run by universities or societies. Example: many IEEE Access articles have APC waivers for certain regions.

How Publication Fees Work

The fee paid by authors (or their institution/grant) to make a paper openly accessible is called APC — Article Processing Charge.

Journal TierTypical APCExamples
Top journals (Nature, Science)$2,000 – $12,000Nature, Cell
Good Scopus/WoS journals$500 – $2,500Elsevier, Springer
MDPI journals$800 – $2,000Sensors, Materials, Energies
Predatory journals$50 – $500(Avoid these)
Free journals (subscription model)₹0Many IEEE, ACM journals
Beginner Tip for India
Many institutions have library subscriptions that waive APC. Your university may also have agreements with publishers. Always ask your supervisor or library before paying fees yourself. Indian researchers can sometimes get APC waivers by applying to journals directly.

Free Options for Beginners

You do NOT need to pay to publish. Many excellent indexed journals are free:

  • IEEE Xplore — many IEEE conference papers are free to submit
  • arXiv.org — free preprint server (physics, CS, engineering)
  • ResearchGate — share papers freely after publication
  • Many Scopus-indexed journals waive fees for authors from developing nations

Predatory / Fake Journals — How to Identify Them

Critical Warning
Predatory journals are fake publications that accept any paper (regardless of quality) just to collect your money. Publishing in them can damage your academic reputation. Always verify before submitting.

Red Flags of Predatory Journals

!
Unsolicited spam emails
"We are pleased to invite your manuscript..." Random emails you never signed up for.
!
Acceptance within hours
Real peer review takes weeks minimum. If accepted in 1–2 days, it is fake.
!
Vague or stolen scope
Journals that claim expertise in every field: "We cover engineering, medicine, arts, social science..."
!
No editorial board or fake names
Editorial board members don't exist or deny being on the board.
!
Not in Scopus or WoS — but they claim to be
Always verify on official databases, not their own website's claim.
!
Low, suspicious fee
₹500–₹2,000 for "publishing" is a major red flag.

How to Verify a Journal is Legitimately Indexed

1
Go to scopus.com/sources — search the exact journal title. If it appears with Source ID, it is Scopus indexed.
2
Go to mjl.clarivate.com (Master Journal List) — search for the journal to verify WoS indexing.
3
Check scimagojr.com — it shows Scopus-indexed journals with quartile rankings.
4
Cross-check UGC CARE list at ugccare.unipune.ac.in if you need it for Indian academic purposes.
5
Google the journal name + "predatory" or check Beall's List (beallslist.net) — maintained list of known predatory publishers.
Chapter 5

Peer Review & Publication Process

What actually happens between submission and publication.

What is Peer Review?

Analogy
Before a movie releases, it goes through preview screenings where critics and the studio review it. They suggest edits, cuts, reshoots. Only after their approval does it release to the public. Peer review is exactly this for research — except the reviewers are anonymous expert scientists/engineers who check your work for mistakes, gaps, and validity.

Step-by-Step: Submission to Publication

1
Choose a Journal
Find a journal that matches your topic, scope, and quality target. Read their "Aims and Scope" page carefully.
2
Format Your Paper
Every journal has an "Author Guidelines" page. Download their template (usually Word or LaTeX). Follow it exactly — font, margins, reference style, abstract length.
3
Check Plagiarism
Run your paper through plagiarism detection software before submission. Aim for below 15% similarity.
4
Submit via Editorial System
Most journals use systems like ScholarOne, Editorial Manager, or OJS. Create an account, upload your paper.
5
Editor's Initial Check (1–4 weeks)
The editor-in-chief reads your paper. If it clearly doesn't fit the journal scope, it gets desk rejected here (without review).
6
Sent to Peer Reviewers (1–3 months)
Editor sends your paper (with your name removed — "blind review") to 2–3 expert reviewers. They read it critically.
7
Editor's Decision
Based on reviewer feedback, you get one of four decisions:
Accept
Rare on first submission. Great news!
Minor Revision
Small changes needed. Usually accepted after.
Major Revision
Significant rework needed. Resubmit for re-review.
Reject
Not accepted. Can submit elsewhere.
8
Revise and Resubmit
If revisions requested, address each reviewer comment carefully. Write a detailed "Response to Reviewers" document explaining what you changed and why.
9
Proofing and Publication
After final acceptance, you get page proofs (formatted version). Check for errors, approve, and your paper goes live — first as "Article in Press" then in a journal issue.

What Happens After Rejection?

Important: Rejection is Normal
Even Nobel Prize winners get papers rejected. The top journals (Nature, Science) reject 90%+ of submissions. Rejection does not mean your work is bad — it often means the fit wasn't right.
1
Read the reviewer feedback carefully and without emotion. Write it down.
2
Improve your paper based on the feedback.
3
Submit to a different (usually slightly lower tier) journal.
4
Repeat. Most papers find a home within 2–4 submissions.

Types of Peer Review

TypeWhat it meansCommon where
Single-blindReviewers know your name; you don't know theirsMost common
Double-blindNeither author nor reviewer knows each otherSocial sciences, some engineering
Open reviewBoth parties know each other; reviews are publicSome modern journals
Post-publicationCommunity reviews after publishingPreprint servers

Plagiarism

Analogy
Submitting someone else's work as your own is plagiarism — like copying a classmate's exam answer sheet and writing your name on it. In research, this is a serious ethical violation that can end careers.

Acceptable Similarity Percentages

SimilarityStatus
0–10%Excellent — Fully original
11–20%Acceptable — minor revisions may help
21–30%Borderline — improve paraphrasing
>30%High risk — likely rejected, must rewrite

Note: Reference lists and quotes are usually excluded from plagiarism calculations. These thresholds vary by institution and journal. Aim for under 15% to be safe.

Turnitin and Plagiarism Tools

Turnitin is the most used software. It compares your paper against billions of web pages, published papers, and student submissions. Your university may give you access. Free alternatives: iThenticate, PlagScan, Grammarly (limited), DupliChecker.

Chapter 6

Writing Your Research Paper

Structure, literature review, writing tips, and how to write even if English isn't your strength.

The Standard Paper Structure

Title
Specific, concise (10–15 words). Should tell what was studied and the main finding or method.
Abstract
150–300 word summary of the entire paper. Covers: problem, method, results, conclusion. Written LAST.
Keywords
5–8 terms that describe your paper's topics. Help search engines find your paper.
Introduction
Why does this problem matter? What exists already? What gap does your work fill? End with your objective.
Literature Review
Survey of previous work on your topic. Show what others did and what was missing that you address.
Methodology
How you did the work. Detailed enough that another researcher can repeat your experiment.
Results
What you found — data, graphs, tables. No interpretation yet. Just the facts.
Discussion
What do the results mean? How do they compare with existing work? Why are they significant?
Conclusion
Summary of contribution. Future work suggestions. No new information here.
References
Every paper, book, website you cited. Follow the journal's citation style precisely.

Literature Review — In Depth

Analogy
Before building a new bridge, an engineer studies all existing bridges: their designs, what worked, what failed, where there are gaps. The literature review is exactly this — you study all previous "bridges" (papers) in your field, then show where your new bridge fills a gap nobody has filled yet.

How to Write a Literature Review (Step by Step)

1
Search Google Scholar, Scopus, IEEE Xplore for papers on your topic from the last 5–10 years.
2
Read abstracts first. Download only the 20–30 most relevant papers.
3
Make a table: Author | Year | Method used | Key finding | Limitation
4
Group papers by theme or approach. Write paragraphs for each group.
5
End with: "However, none of these studies addressed [your specific gap], which is the focus of this work."

How to Read a Paper Efficiently

1
Read Title + Abstract (2 min)
Decide if it's relevant before investing time.
2
Read Introduction + Conclusion (5 min)
Understand what problem they solved and what they found.
3
Scan Figures and Tables (3 min)
Results are often best understood visually first.
4
Read Methodology if needed
Only dive into methods if you need to replicate or compare.

Writing Tips for Non-Native English Speakers

Practical Advice

Most research papers are not written in literary English — they follow a very formal, structured style that you can learn with practice.

1
Use Grammarly (free version)
For grammar and spell check while writing.
2
Read and copy sentence patterns from published papers
Notice phrases like "The results indicate that..." "This paper proposes..." Use similar patterns (not copy the content).
3
Write first, polish later
Write rough ideas in any language, then translate and refine. Don't try to write perfectly first.
4
Use DeepL or ChatGPT for language polishing
After writing in your own words, use AI tools to improve phrasing — but always understand and verify what you submit.
5
Ask your supervisor to review
That's part of their role. A good supervisor will help you improve language in the first few papers.

How to Select a Research Topic

1
Start with what you know
Your final year project, coursework area, or lab specialty.
2
Search trending topics
Look at recent IEEE, Scopus papers from 2022–2024 in your field. What's being actively studied?
3
Find a gap
After reading 20 papers, what problem did they all fail to solve? That's your opportunity.
4
Check feasibility
Can you do this with available tools, equipment, data, and time? Be realistic.
5
Discuss with supervisor
Always validate your topic with a mentor before investing months of work.
Chapter 7

Research Ethics & Authorship

The rules of honest science — and what happens when they are broken.

The Core Ethics Rules

✓
Honesty
Report your results exactly as they are. Never "adjust" data to fit your hypothesis.
✓
No Plagiarism
Always cite sources. Never present someone else's ideas or words as your own — even paraphrased ideas need citation.
✓
No Fabrication or Falsification
Inventing data that doesn't exist (fabrication) or manipulating results (falsification) are severe misconduct. Career-ending offenses.
✓
No Duplicate Submission
You cannot submit the same paper to two journals at the same time. Wait for one to decide before submitting elsewhere.
✓
Declare Conflicts of Interest
If funding came from a company, or you have a personal relationship with someone involved — disclose it.

Authorship — Who Goes on the Paper?

RoleWho is it?Responsibilities
First AuthorThe person who did most of the work — usually the studentLeads the writing; responsible for majority of content
Corresponding AuthorHandles all communication with the journalReceives acceptance/rejection, submits on behalf of all
Co-authorAnyone who made significant contributionContributed to design, analysis, writing, or interpretation
Ghost Authorship and Gift Authorship

Gift authorship = adding a professor's name who contributed nothing just to help get published. Unethical.

Ghost authorship = leaving out someone who did real work. Also unethical.

Both violate international publication ethics (COPE guidelines). Only add authors who genuinely contributed.

Self-Plagiarism — A Common Trap for Students

Reusing large sections of your own previously published work without citation is also considered plagiarism (called "text recycling" or "self-plagiarism"). Always cite your own previous papers if you use content from them. A maximum of 10–20% overlap from your own work is typically acceptable.

COPE — Committee on Publication Ethics

The international governing body for research ethics in publishing. Most reputable journals follow COPE guidelines. You can report ethical violations at publicationethics.org.

ORCID — The Most Important One

Create your ORCID at orcid.org — it's free and takes 5 minutes. Link all your papers to it. Many journals will ask for your ORCID ID during submission. It ensures your papers are always linked to you, even if your name is common (imagine 50 researchers all named "Rahul Kumar" — ORCID distinguishes each one).

Google Scholar Profile

Once you have a paper published, go to scholar.google.com, create a profile with your institutional email. Google Scholar automatically finds and links papers under your name. Your profile shows: list of papers, total citations, H-index, i10-index (papers with 10+ citations).

Tip
Set your Google Scholar profile to "public" so anyone searching your name can find your work. This is your global academic business card.

How Citations Work

Analogy
When a politician gives a speech, journalists quote them. The more a politician is quoted, the more influential they are considered. Citations work the same way: when other researchers reference your paper in their work, that is a citation. More citations = more influence.

Citations also create a chain of knowledge. Each paper acknowledges the "shoulders of giants" it stands on. Referencing properly tells readers: "I didn't invent this idea — I built on these existing works."

Analogy
A conference is like a science festival. Researchers from all over gather at one place (or online), present their latest work in short talks or poster sessions, exchange ideas, and network. Think of it as a TED Talk for engineers, but where everyone is presenting their own research.

IEEE Conferences

IEEE (Institute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers) is the world's largest technical professional organization. Their conferences are well-respected in engineering. Papers published in IEEE conferences appear on IEEE Xplore and are often Scopus indexed.

Examples: IEEE ICIT, IEEE ICRA, IEEE ICCV, IEEE PEDS. Check upcoming conferences at conference.ieee.org

Springer Conferences (LNCS)

Springer publishes conference proceedings under the "Lecture Notes in Computer Science" (LNCS) and similar series. Well-regarded and often Scopus indexed. Good option for CS, AI, and engineering topics.

Register and present

Chapter 10

Citations & Referencing Styles

IEEE, APA, MLA — why they matter and how they work.

Why Referencing Matters

Analogy
When you cook from a recipe you found online, crediting the original chef is basic honesty. In research, citing your sources is the same — it acknowledges whose ideas you built upon, allows readers to verify your claims, and gives credit where it belongs.

Citation Styles Compared

StyleUsed inIn-text formatReference list format
IEEEEngineering, Computer Science, Electronics[1], [2], [3] — numbered in order of appearanceNumbered list at end
APAPsychology, Social sciences, Management(Smith, 2022) or Smith (2022)Alphabetical by last name
MLAHumanities, Literature, Arts(Smith 45) — author + page"Works Cited" alphabetical

IEEE Style Example

In text: "The method was first proposed in [5] and later improved in [6]."

In references: [5] A. Kumar, B. Sharma, and C. Singh, "A new method for image segmentation," IEEE Trans. Image Process., vol. 32, pp. 112–125, Jan. 2023.

APA Style Example

In text: "The efficiency was improved by 23% (Kumar et al., 2023)."

In references: Kumar, A., Sharma, B., & Singh, C. (2023). A new method for image segmentation. IEEE Transactions on Image Processing, 32, 112–125.

Reference Management Tools

Zotero (Free)
Browser extension + desktop app. Collects papers automatically, generates citations in any style. Best free option.
Mendeley (Free)
Elsevier's reference manager. Great for organizing and annotating PDFs. Also has social network features.
EndNote
Powerful but expensive. Often available via university licenses.
Google Scholar + Copy cite
Click "Cite" under any paper on Google Scholar to instantly get IEEE/APA/MLA formatted citation.
Best Practice
Use Zotero from Day 1. As you read papers, save them to Zotero. When writing your paper, insert citations with one click. It saves enormous time and eliminates formatting errors.
Chapter 11

Your Complete Roadmap

From knowing nothing to publishing your first paper.

Common Mistakes Beginners Make

Avoid These
✗
Submitting to predatory journals
Always verify Scopus/WoS indexing before submission.
✗
High plagiarism (copy-pasting)
Every statement must be paraphrased. Even one paragraph copy-pasted can get your paper rejected.
✗
Ignoring the author guidelines
Wrong font, wrong reference style = instant rejection by editors.
✗
Not doing enough literature review
Reviewers know the field. If you miss 5 key papers, they will notice and reject.
✗
Giving up after first rejection
Every researcher faces rejection. Revise and resubmit. That's the process.
✗
No ORCID or researcher profiles
Create your profiles before or at time of first submission.

The Beginner's Publication Timeline

Month 1–2
Foundation Phase

Learn what research is. Create ORCID. Create Google Scholar profile. Learn to search on Google Scholar and IEEE Xplore. Read 20 papers in your area of interest.

Month 2–4
Topic Selection & Deep Reading

Identify a specific research gap. Read 30–50 targeted papers. Discuss with supervisor. Define your research question and methodology.

Month 3–6
Experimentation & Data Collection

Do the actual research work — experiments, simulations, surveys, or data analysis. Document everything meticulously.

Month 5–7
Writing Phase

Write the paper section by section. Start with Methodology (easiest), then Results, Introduction, Literature Review. Write Abstract last.

Month 7
Review & Submission

Plagiarism check, grammar check, supervisor review, format per journal guidelines. Submit.

Month 7–12
Review Process

Wait for reviewer feedback. Respond to revisions. Resubmit. Accept minor/major revisions as part of the process.

Month 9–14
Publication!

Accepted. Proofread. Published online. Share on ResearchGate, update Google Scholar profile. Celebrate — you're now a published researcher.

  • ☐Verified target journal is Scopus/WoS indexed
  • ☐Downloaded journal's Author Guidelines template
  • ☐Run plagiarism check (below 15%)
  • ☐Grammar checked with Grammarly
  • ☐Supervisor has reviewed final draft
  • ☐All authors have approved the submission

Low-Cost / Free Publishing Options for Beginners

1
IEEE conferences
Many IEEE conferences have registration fees of $100–200. But proceedings are indexed and respected.
2
MDPI journals
APC waivers available for authors from low-income countries. Apply during submission.
3
Subscription journals (free to publish)
Many top journals (IEEE Trans., Elsevier without OA) are free to publish in — readers pay, not authors.
4
University-funded APC
Ask your institution — many Indian universities have funds specifically for publication fees.
5
arXiv + later journal submission
Post on arXiv.org immediately for free. Submit to a journal afterward. Your work is visible while under review.

Final Words of Encouragement

Research is a marathon, not a sprint. Every expert you look up to started exactly where you are — with no papers, no citations, no knowledge of how any of this worked. The only difference is they started. Your first paper will be imperfect. Submit it anyway. The second will be better. The tenth will be excellent. The journey begins with curiosity and courage — you already have both.

Good luck, researcher. The world needs what you will discover.
